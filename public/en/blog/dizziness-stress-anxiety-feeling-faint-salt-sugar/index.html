Dizziness, stress and feeling faint: why a little salt, sugar or honey can sometimes help

A simple guide for moments when stress, poor sleep or intense mental work make you feel dizzy, weak or close to fainting.

Sometimes, during stress, poor sleep or intense mental work, the body starts sending strange signals. You stand up and feel dizzy. Your legs feel weak. You feel like you may faint. Light bothers you. Your heart beats faster. You get scared, and anxiety becomes even stronger.

This happens more often than people think. It does not automatically mean something serious, but it should not be ignored if it repeats. The nervous system may be tired, blood sugar may drop, blood pressure may change, breathing may become faster, muscles may stay tense, and the brain reads all of this as danger.

Why can the feeling of fainting appear?

The feeling of fainting often appears when the brain temporarily receives less blood flow, oxygen or energy than it needs. This can happen after poor sleep, skipped meals, too little water, long screen work, heat or a stressful period.

Anxiety can amplify everything. When you get scared, breathing changes, adrenaline rises, the heart beats differently and the body feels unsafe. Sometimes the main issue is not danger, but exhaustion and an over-reactive nervous system.

Why can a little salt help?

Salt helps the body hold water and may support blood pressure in some people, especially after sweating, low fluid intake, low food intake or naturally lower blood pressure. We are not talking about a lot of salt. A small salty snack or electrolytes may be enough for some people.

Important: if you have high blood pressure, kidney disease, heart problems, swelling or a medical recommendation to reduce salt, do not do this without your doctor’s advice.

Why can a little sugar or honey help?

If you skipped a meal, drank coffee on an empty stomach or worked for many hours without a break, blood sugar can drop or fluctuate. This can bring shakiness, weakness, sweating, hunger, anxiety, dizziness or brain fog.

In that moment, a small amount of sugar, honey, juice or a sweet snack can help some people recover faster. The idea is not to eat a lot of sweets, but to have something quick in case energy suddenly drops.

If you have diabetes or known blood sugar problems, follow your doctor’s plan.

Why can sunglasses help?

During stress, migraine, fatigue or after long screen time, strong light can become difficult to tolerate. Some people feel headache, nausea, dizziness or irritation from light. Sunglasses can reduce visual stimulation and help calm the nervous system in that moment.

But if light sensitivity appears suddenly, is very strong, or comes with a severe headache, fever, vision problems or confusion, it needs medical evaluation.

What can you do in the moment?

Stop. Sit or lie down. If possible, raise your legs a little. Breathe more slowly. Take a few sips of water. If you have eaten very little, take a small amount of sugar, honey or a snack. If you have been sweating or you know your blood pressure is low, a small salty snack or electrolytes may help.

Do not drive and do not keep standing if you feel you may faint. The body is asking for a pause.

What should you do next?

If it happened once after poor sleep, hunger, stress or heat, it may be a signal to hydrate, eat regularly, sleep and reduce overload. But if it repeats, if you faint, or if you have strong palpitations, chest pain, shortness of breath, one-sided weakness, confusion or an unusual headache, seek medical help.
